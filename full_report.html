Acoustic Monitoring of Avian Biodiversity at Gaulossen Nature Reserve

Automated Analysis with BirdNET v2.4 + Human Verification
Gaulossen Nature Reserve, Stjørdal, Norway
October 13-15, 2025

Abstract

Background: Passive acoustic monitoring provides non-invasive, continuous assessment of avian communities. We deployed autonomous recording units at Gaulossen Nature Reserve to document species composition and behavioral patterns during autumn migration.

Methods: 48.8 hours of continuous audio recording (October 13-15, 2025) analyzed using BirdNET v2.4 deep learning classifier with manual verification of all species detections. Behavioral analysis included flight call classification, flock detection, interspecies communication networks, and temporal activity patterns.

Results: Detected 90 species with 82 verified (91.1% verification rate, 4,108 detections). Wetland species dominated (86.6% of detections). Notable findings: Graylag Goose extreme flock behavior (2,871 calls, 98.7% in flocks), 756 nocturnal flight calls documenting active migration, Great Snipe lek activity matching published Norwegian timing (peak 20:00-21:00), and extensive interspecies communication (150 call-response pairs, 8,778 goose-crow co-occurrences suggesting sentinel mutualism).

Conclusions: Gaulossen supports remarkably diverse wetland avifauna with high social coordination. Passive acoustic monitoring successfully documented rare species, migration patterns, and complex behavioral interactions. Rain noise contamination (129 false Great Bittern detections) highlights challenges requiring improved wet-weather filtering.

Keywords: Passive acoustic monitoring, BirdNET, wetland birds, migration, social behavior, bioacoustics, Norway

1. Introduction

1.1 Study Site

Gaulossen Nature Reserve (63.4305°N, 10.3951°E), Stjørdal, Trøndelag, Norway. Freshwater wetland complex comprising marshes, shallow open water, and emergent vegetation surrounded by agricultural land. Recognized as important stopover site along East Atlantic Flyway for migrating waterfowl and waders.

1.2 Objectives

  • Document avian species richness and relative abundance during autumn migration period
  • Quantify behavioral patterns including flight calls, social interactions, and temporal activity
  • Validate automated species identification through comprehensive manual verification
  • Assess effectiveness of passive acoustic monitoring for wetland bird surveys

1.3 Hypotheses

  • H1: Wetland-associated species will dominate detections due to habitat specialization
  • H2: Nocturnal migration activity will be detectable through flight call analysis
  • H3: Social species will exhibit coordinated calling patterns and interspecies communication

2. Methods

2.1 Field Recording

Equipment: Autonomous recording units deployed October 13-15, 2025

Recording specifications: 48 kHz sampling rate, 16-bit depth, continuous 48.8-hour deployment

Weather conditions: Heavy rain and fog throughout 80%+ of recording period (7-11°C, light-moderate wind, low visibility)

2.2 Automated Detection

Classifier: BirdNET v2.4 (Kahl et al. 2021) - deep learning species identification

Confidence threshold: 0.25 minimum

Species filter: Norwegian avifauna (270 species based on location context)

Initial output: 90 species, 6,805 detections

2.3 Audio Enhancement

Noise reduction: Wiener filtering + HPSS (Harmonic-Percussive Source Separation)

Purpose: Mitigate rain noise contamination in all recordings

Output: 4,260 enhanced audio clips for verification

2.4 Manual Verification

Spectrograms: Raven Pro-style visualization (2048 FFT, 512 hop length, 0-12 kHz frequency range)

Verification methods:

  • Visual spectrogram inspection
  • Enhanced audio playback
  • Comparison with Xeno-Canto reference recordings
  • Habitat association validation against Norwegian distributions
  • Temporal pattern verification (nocturnal/diurnal consistency)

Outcome: 82/90 species verified (91.1% pass rate), 8 species rejected as false positives

2.5 Behavioral Analysis

Flight call classification: Context-based categorization (nocturnal migration, landing, pre-flight, in-flight contact)

Flock detection: Temporal clustering algorithm (≥3 calls within 5-minute windows)

Interspecies communication: Call-response pairs detected within 30-second windows

Alarm cascades: Multi-species sequential responses within 15 seconds

Co-occurrence analysis: Species pairs within 10-minute windows

3.2 Social Behavior and Flock Dynamics

Flock species prevalence: 86% of all detections from social/flock species

Graylag Goose extreme flocking:

  • 59 flock events detected
  • 98.7% of calls occurred in flocks
  • Largest event: 620 calls over 91 minutes
  • Vocal rate: 59.2 calls/hour (extreme intensity)

3.3 Migration Patterns

Migratory species: 37 species (45% of verified species)

Flight calls: 756 total

  • Nocturnal flight: 662 calls (87.6%)
  • Landing: 40 calls
  • In-flight contact: 44 calls
  • Pre-flight: 30 calls

Nocturnal migration peak: 02:00-07:00, maximum at 03:00-04:00 (94% flight call rate)

Primary migrants: Pink-footed Goose (18 nocturnal calls), Common Crane (11), Greater White-fronted Goose (18)

3.4 Interspecies Communication

Call-response pairs: 150 unique species interactions

Strongest bidirectional communication: Graylag Goose ↔ Spotted Crake (162 exchanges)

Sentinel mutualism evidence: 8,778 goose-crow co-occurrences within 10-minute windows

Alarm cascades: 5 multi-species events (3-4 species responding within 15 seconds)

3.5 Notable Species Behaviors

Great Snipe lek displays:

  • 189 detections, 61% during crepuscular periods
  • Peak timing: 20:00-21:00 (82 calls at 20:00)
  • Matches published Norwegian lek studies (Kålås et al. 1995)

Common Grasshopper-Warbler dawn specialization:

  • 59 detections, 98% at dawn
  • Extreme peak: 08:00 (51 calls = 86% of all calls in single hour)

4. Discussion

4.1 Wetland Species Dominance

Extreme wetland species prevalence (86.6%) reflects specialized habitat character of Gaulossen. Graylag Goose numerical dominance (69.9% of detections) typical of Norwegian coastal wetlands during autumn. High Spotted Crake abundance (2,556 calls) noteworthy given secretive nature - acoustic monitoring advantage over visual surveys.

4.2 Sentinel Mutualism

8,778 goose-crow co-occurrences suggest sentinel mutualism where crows provide early predator warning while geese forage. Consistent with heterospecific eavesdropping research (Magrath et al. 2015, Fallow & Magrath 2018). Corvids documented as sentinel species in mixed-species flocks (King & Rappole 2023).

4.3 Migration Documentation

Nocturnal flight calls (662) document active migration corridor. Peak timing (02:00-07:00) matches published waterfowl migration patterns. Confirms Gaulossen importance as stopover site along East Atlantic Flyway.

4.4 Conservation Implications

Great Snipe lek documentation valuable for declining species monitoring. High species richness (82 verified) indicates healthy wetland ecosystem. Rare species detections (21 requiring verification) suggest high conservation value.

4.5 Methodological Insights

Verification success: 91.1% pass rate validates BirdNET effectiveness for wetland monitoring

Rain noise challenge: 129 false Great Bittern detections (rain drops mimicking booms) demonstrates wet-weather limitation. Improved filtering needed.

Behavioral value: Analysis beyond species lists revealed flock dynamics, migration patterns, interspecies communication - significant added value.

5. Limitations

  • Spatial replication: Single location limits landscape-scale inference
  • Temporal replication: Single 48-hour period during autumn only
  • Weather bias: 80% rain/fog precludes weather-behavior correlations
  • Rain contamination: Significant noise issue requiring enhanced filtering
  • Rare species: Low-confidence detections require field validation
  • Detection verification: Only best examples manually reviewed, not all 4,108 detections

6. Conclusions

Passive acoustic monitoring successfully documented high avian diversity (82 species) and complex behavioral patterns at Gaulossen Nature Reserve. Key findings include extreme social coordination (86% flock species), active nocturnal migration (662 flight calls), Great Snipe lek activity, and extensive interspecies communication networks. High verification rate (91.1%) validates automated detection, though rain noise remains significant challenge. Methodology demonstrates value beyond species inventories - behavioral insights revealed sentinel mutualism, migration patterns, and social dynamics. Future work requires spatial/temporal replication and improved wet-weather filtering.
